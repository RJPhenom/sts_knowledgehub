The demand for machine learning engineers and AI specialists has outpaced supply by a wide margin for several years, and the gap is not closing at the rate many organisations expected. The assumption that universities would produce enough trained talent to meet enterprise demand has proven optimistic. The organisations managing this constraint well are doing something different from the ones waiting for the market to correct.

The first adjustment is skill decomposition. "We need to hire an AI engineer" is often an imprecise statement of a more specific set of requirements. The skills needed to build and maintain a RAG pipeline for internal documents are different from the skills needed to design a fine-tuning pipeline, which are different again from the skills needed to evaluate and procure AI vendor products. Organisations that break down the actual skill requirements tend to find that they have more internal capability than they thought, concentrated in unexpected places.

Upskilling existing staff has produced better returns than most organisations predicted. Data engineers who understand the infrastructure side can learn the AI-specific patterns. Software engineers can learn prompt engineering and LLM integration. The gap is real, but it is narrower than a pure hiring-market analysis suggests. The constraint is usually structured learning time and organisational commitment, not raw capability.

For capabilities that are genuinely unavailable internally, the choice between building and buying has shifted toward buying in more cases than before. The quality of available AI products has improved to the point that custom development is harder to justify for commodity use cases. Reserving scarce AI talent for the problems where custom development genuinely creates competitive advantage — and using well-chosen products for everything else — is the allocation that makes sense for most enterprises today.
