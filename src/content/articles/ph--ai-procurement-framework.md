The AI vendor landscape has never been more crowded, and the quality variance between vendors has never been higher. A polished demo and a credible-sounding technical explanation can obscure fundamental weaknesses in a product that will only become visible after you have signed a contract and begun integration. A structured evaluation process is not optional anymore — it is the minimum standard of due diligence.

Start with use-case specificity. Vendors who cannot describe in concrete terms which of your problems they solve, with what accuracy, and under what conditions, are not ready for enterprise deployment regardless of what their pitch deck says. Ask for reference customers in your industry who have deployed the specific capability you are evaluating, not their most impressive customer in an adjacent space. Talk to those references without the vendor present.

The evaluation environment matters as much as the evaluation criteria. Testing on your own data — not a sanitised demo dataset, but a representative sample of the messy, edge-case-laden data your system will actually process — is non-negotiable. Build a test set that includes the failure modes you most need to avoid. Measure accuracy, latency, and cost on that set, not on the vendor's benchmarks.

Contractual protections are the final layer of due diligence. Data handling and residency provisions should be explicit, not implied. SLAs need to reflect the actual criticality of the system, not a generic template. Exit provisions — what happens to your data, your model fine-tunes, and your configurations if you switch vendors — should be negotiated upfront, when you still have leverage.
