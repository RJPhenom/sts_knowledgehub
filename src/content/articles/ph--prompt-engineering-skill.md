Prompt engineering has an image problem. The name sounds like a workaround — something you do because you cannot do the "real" engineering. This perception is both wrong and expensive. The organisations that have treated prompt development as a serious engineering discipline are getting meaningfully better results from the same models as those who treat it as trial and error.

What separates good prompt engineering from bad is the same thing that separates good software engineering from bad: rigor, measurement, and iteration. A good prompt development process starts with a clear task specification and a representative evaluation set. Changes to the prompt are measured against that evaluation set, not eyeballed on a handful of examples. The evaluation set is updated as new failure modes are discovered. The prompt is versioned and documented like any other piece of production code.

The specific techniques — chain-of-thought, structured output formatting, role specification, few-shot example selection — are secondary to this underlying discipline. Any technique applied without measurement is guesswork. The techniques that matter most will be different for different tasks, and the only way to know which ones matter for your task is to measure.

The career implication is real. As enterprises build more LLM-dependent systems, the ability to design, evaluate, and maintain effective prompts is becoming a distinct and valued competency. It sits at the intersection of technical understanding, domain knowledge, and communication clarity in ways that are hard to automate and hard to outsource. Teams that invest in developing this capability internally are accumulating an advantage that compounds over time.
