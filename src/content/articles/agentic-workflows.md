The term "agentic AI" has acquired the same overloaded quality that "cloud" had in 2010 — everyone is using it, fewer people mean the same thing by it, and the hype is running well ahead of the deployments. That said, the underlying capability shift is real, and the enterprises that understand it clearly will have a genuine edge over those chasing the marketing.

An agentic workflow is, at its core, a system in which an AI model takes sequences of actions — not just generating text, but calling tools, reading and writing data, making decisions, and handing off to other agents or humans based on intermediate results. The key distinction from traditional automation is that the sequencing is determined dynamically by the model, not hardcoded by a developer. This makes agents far more flexible than RPA, but also far harder to predict and audit.

The enterprise use cases that are working today are narrower than the demos suggest. Research and synthesis tasks — gathering information from multiple internal sources, summarising, and flagging inconsistencies — are a strong fit. So are multi-step triage workflows where the number of possible paths is too large for a traditional decision tree but the stakes are low enough that errors can be caught and corrected. Customer-facing agents with real financial or legal consequences are still early.

The infrastructure question is underappreciated. Agentic systems require robust tool integrations, reliable memory management, and careful attention to error handling and retry logic. The model is only one component. Teams that underinvest in the surrounding infrastructure tend to get systems that work brilliantly in demos and fail unpredictably in production.
