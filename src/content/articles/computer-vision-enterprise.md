For most of its history, computer vision lived in research labs and niche industrial settings — quality control on factory floors, satellite image analysis, medical imaging. The idea that it might one day be processing invoices, verifying identity documents, or reading handwritten forms in a mid-sized accounting firm would have seemed far-fetched. It no longer does.

The shift has been driven by two converging forces: the dramatic improvement in off-the-shelf vision models, and the equally dramatic drop in the cost of deploying them. What once required a team of ML engineers and months of training data can now be configured in days using pre-trained foundation models fine-tuned on a few hundred examples. For enterprise buyers, this changes the calculus entirely.

The most common entry point is document processing. Accounts payable teams drowning in purchase orders, invoices, and receipts are finding that a well-configured vision pipeline can extract, validate, and route documents with accuracy rates that exceed manual processing — and at a fraction of the cost. The human role shifts from data entry to exception handling, which is a better use of their time anyway.

What the benchmarks rarely capture is the operational fragility that comes with early deployments. Vision models are sensitive to document layout changes, scanning quality, and edge cases that simply didn't appear in the training set. The organisations seeing the best results are those that treat the first deployment as a learning exercise — instrumented heavily, monitored closely, and improved iteratively — rather than a finished product. The technology is genuinely ready. The question is whether the deployment practice is.
