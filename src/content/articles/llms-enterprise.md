Two years into the enterprise LLM wave, the pattern is becoming clearer. A small number of use cases are generating consistent, measurable value. A much larger number of pilots have stalled, been quietly shelved, or are still in the evaluation phase with no clear path to production. Understanding what separates the two groups is more useful than any benchmark comparison.

The highest-performing deployments share a few characteristics. They are narrowly scoped: the model is doing one thing well, not ten things adequately. They have a clear feedback loop: someone is reviewing outputs, logging errors, and feeding that information back into prompt and retrieval improvements. And they have a human in the loop at the right point — not everywhere, which kills efficiency, but at the specific junctures where errors are costly enough to warrant review.

Internal knowledge retrieval is the use case with the most consistent returns. Organisations with large bodies of unstructured documentation — technical manuals, policy libraries, prior project reports — are finding that a well-built RAG system meaningfully reduces the time employees spend searching for information and increases the quality of what they find. The implementation is not trivial, but the value proposition is direct and measurable.

Code assistance has also crossed the line from experimental to standard practice at most technology-forward organisations. The productivity gains are real and relatively easy to measure. The risks — insecure code generation, copyright exposure, overreliance — are real too, and the organisations managing them well have explicit policies, not just good intentions. Customer-facing use cases remain harder. The expectation gap between what LLMs can reliably do and what customers expect them to do is still significant, and the reputational cost of visible failures is high.
