Most organisations have a rough sense that manual data entry is expensive. Few have quantified it carefully enough to make a compelling internal case for change. The gap between "we know this is wasteful" and "here is the precise cost" is often what stands between a stalled automation proposal and an approved one.

The fully-loaded cost of a data entry error is almost always higher than it appears. There is the direct labour cost of the person entering the data. There is the cost of the downstream systems that consume and act on that data — if the data is wrong, those actions are wrong too. There is the reconciliation cost when discrepancies surface. And there is the opportunity cost of what the person could have been doing instead. When you add these up across a finance team processing thousands of transactions a month, the numbers tend to be startling.

The exercise of building this cost model has value independent of whether you end up automating anything. It forces a conversation about data quality standards, process ownership, and error rates that most organisations have never had explicitly. Teams that go through this process often discover that their actual error rate is significantly higher than the rate they assumed — because errors that don't cause immediate problems tend not to get logged.

The automation ROI calculation then becomes straightforward. You are not replacing labour with software; you are replacing a known error rate and a known labour cost with a lower error rate and a lower ongoing cost, while freeing your team for work that actually requires their judgment.
