Automation ROI is one of the most frequently miscalculated numbers in enterprise technology. The errors run in both directions: projects that look marginal on a narrow cost basis turn out to be highly valuable when indirect benefits are included, and projects with impressive headline savings disappoint when implementation and maintenance costs are properly accounted for. A rigorous framework prevents both errors.

The most common measurement mistake is counting full-time equivalent hours saved and multiplying by an average salary. This calculation is almost always wrong. It is wrong because it assumes the hours freed up disappear from the cost base, when in most cases they are redeployed. It is wrong because it uses average salaries when the actual work being automated may be concentrated in a higher or lower cost tier. And it is wrong because it ignores the indirect costs — support, exceptions, edge cases — that automation creates rather than eliminates.

Better metrics start with process output, not labour input. How many transactions are processed per unit time, at what error rate, and with what cycle time? If automation improves these metrics in ways that have downstream business value — faster close cycles, lower error-driven costs, more consistent compliance — those are the numbers to present. They are also harder to dispute than a labour substitution argument.

The implementation and run cost model needs to be fully loaded. Development time, integration complexity, testing, deployment, and the ongoing cost of monitoring and maintaining a system that will break in new and creative ways as the upstream environment changes — these need to be in the denominator. Projects that skip this step tend to discover their true costs in year two, at which point they are already committed.
