The cloud vs. on-premise decision for AI workloads has become more nuanced than it was two years ago. The default answer — use the cloud for flexibility, on-premise for compliance-sensitive workloads — still holds in many cases, but the economics and the risk profiles have shifted enough that a more careful analysis is warranted for any significant deployment.

Cloud inference has gotten cheaper and the major providers have significantly improved their data residency and compliance certifications. For organisations whose primary concern was data sovereignty, many of the obstacles have been reduced, though not eliminated. Highly regulated sectors — certain healthcare applications, public sector workloads, financial services in jurisdictions with strict data localisation requirements — still face constraints that cloud alone cannot address.

On-premise AI has gotten more accessible. The availability of capable smaller models that run efficiently on commodity hardware, combined with maturing inference serving infrastructure, has lowered the bar for running meaningful AI workloads without cloud dependency. The capital cost and operational overhead are real, but for organisations with existing data centre capacity and the technical staff to manage it, the total cost of ownership calculation is closer than it has historically been.

The latency question is often the deciding factor in applications where it matters. Real-time customer-facing applications with sub-100ms response requirements and high query volumes may find that on-premise or edge deployment is the only economically viable option. Batch and async workloads have much more flexibility. Map your latency requirements before you map your infrastructure options — the answer often becomes obvious once you do.
