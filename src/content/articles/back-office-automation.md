Back-office automation rarely makes headlines, which is precisely why it is so far ahead of the more visible AI applications. While the industry debates the implications of AI-generated content and autonomous agents, the unglamorous work of processing invoices, onboarding new employees, and running compliance checks has been quietly handed to software. The transition is well underway and largely irreversible.

Accounts payable is the canonical example. The workflow — receive invoice, match to purchase order, route for approval, schedule payment — is sufficiently standardised that automation handles the majority of cases without human intervention at most organisations that have made the investment. Exception handling still requires people, but the ratio of exceptions to straight-through processing continues to improve as the models see more data.

HR onboarding is further along than most people realise. Document collection, identity verification, benefits enrolment, system provisioning — each step in the new employee journey involves structured data collection and rule-based processing that maps well to automation. The human element that remains is meaningful: relationship building, culture transmission, answering the questions that aren't in any document. Automation is not eliminating the HR function, but it is concentrating it on the parts that actually require human judgment.

Compliance monitoring is the newest entrant. Continuous monitoring of transactions, communications, and system activity for policy violations and regulatory red flags was previously a sampling exercise — you could check some of the things some of the time. AI-driven monitoring is shifting this toward comprehensive coverage, with humans reviewing flagged cases rather than conducting the initial scan.
