Canada's Artificial Intelligence and Data Act has been in development long enough that some organisations have started to treat it as perpetually pending. That was a reasonable stance eighteen months ago. It is no longer reasonable now. The regulatory direction is clear, the compliance timeline is tightening, and organisations that have done nothing are beginning to fall meaningfully behind those that have.

The Act establishes a risk-tiered framework for AI systems — a structure familiar from European regulation but adapted to the Canadian context. High-impact systems, defined primarily by their potential for significant harm to individuals or groups, face the most stringent requirements: impact assessments, transparency obligations, human oversight requirements, and incident reporting. The definition of "high-impact" is broader than many organisations initially assume; it captures a significant portion of AI systems used in financial services, healthcare, hiring, and public services.

For most enterprises, the immediate compliance priority is inventory and classification. You cannot manage regulatory risk for AI systems you have not catalogued. Many organisations are discovering, through this process, that they have deployed AI in more places than their central IT function knows about — through shadow IT, vendor-bundled AI features, and departmental initiatives that never went through a formal procurement process.

The organisations best positioned for AIDA compliance are those that have been building internal AI governance frameworks regardless of regulatory pressure. If you have a model registry, an impact assessment process, and clear ownership for AI systems, the compliance work is largely documentation. If you are starting from scratch, the timeline is real and the resourcing requirement is significant.
